Klang Valley
Flood Watch

A live map of flood telemetry for Selangor, Kuala Lumpur and Putrajaya — river levels, rainfall, warning sirens and flood-depth gauges, from every station the Department of Irrigation and Drainage publishes, on one map that refreshes itself.

The official portals each hold a piece of it and none of them plot it together, so answering "is the river near me rising" meant reading three sites and a table. This joins them, works out which stations are actually climbing towards their own danger marks, and shows that instead.

Not an official warning channel. This site is not affiliated with JPS. It re-publishes public data and can be wrong, stale or down — in an emergency, follow JPS and NADMA directly.
If you are in danger, call 999, or the flood emergency line for your state: civildefence.gov.my (APM).

What puts a station on alert

The On alert panel and the strip along the top list a station for one of three reasons. Nothing else on this map raises an alarm.

HAPPENING NOW A river at or past its danger mark, or a siren sounding. Observed, not predicted. FORECAST A river climbing fast enough to reach its own danger mark within 3 hours — at least 0.1 m an hour, with its last three readings not dipping. Anything slower is sensor rounding rather than a climb; anything further out is a guess with too little behind it. NOT CURRENT Either of the above, from a station that has since gone quiet — offline, or a reading over a day old. It stays on the list rather than vanishing, because a river sitting at its danger mark whose telemetry just died is the last thing that should quietly disappear. It sorts to the bottom and is left out of the counts.

Every mark is the station's own, as published by JPS, so a small drain and a large river are each measured against what floods them — being “high” is meaningless without knowing high for what. Rainfall, flood-depth gauges and cameras are drawn on the map but never raise an alert. Sensors you have ignored raise none either, and the panel keeps saying how many are silenced.

Reading the map

One pin per place. The glyph says what kind of sensor is there, the colour says how it is doing. Type colours are never a traffic light, and traffic-light colours never mean anything but status.

The five kinds — water level, rainfall, siren, flood-depth gauge, camera — each in its own colour, none of which is ever used for status. A river past its alert, warning and danger marks. At danger it fills red and keeps a pulsing halo, and it is drawn over everything else. A red ring: climbing towards its danger mark, and forecast to reach it within 3 hours. 4 Several sensors on one mast, counted. Hover or open it to see the area they were grouped from. If any of them is signalling, the pin takes that status colour instead. Grey and faded: offline, or reporting nothing. Never coloured — a station with no reading must not look confident. 12 Pins too close together to draw apart, with how many. Zoom in or click to open them out.

The two heatmaps answer different questions, so only one runs at a time. Water level scores every river and flood gauge against its own marks — yellow past alert, orange past warning, red at danger — and paints nothing below the first mark, because a map that is warm everywhere says nothing.

alert → warning → danger light → moderate → heavy → violent

Rainfall uses the intensity classes JPS publishes — above 0, 10, 30 and 60 mm in the last hour. It says where the water is arriving, not where it is standing. Both are smoothed over a few kilometres, because flooding is catchment-scale, and both show the strongest reading in an area rather than adding neighbours together.

Where this data comes from

Public Infobanjir JPS Malaysia. Water levels and flood thresholds. Takes priority wherever it carries a station, so a reading matches the national portal.
JPS Selangor Infobanjir Selangor stations, and the only source here for cameras, sirens and flood gauges. Supplies the coordinates every pin is drawn at.
JPS Wilayah Persekutuan (SPHTN) Kuala Lumpur and Putrajaya — water level and rainfall stations the Selangor feed does not cover, with their coordinates.

Each station popup names the feed its reading came from. Where the feeds disagree, the newest reading wins, except that Public Infobanjir's figure is preferred outright.

        How it is worked out

        Each station scores its reading against its own thresholds, so a small stream at its limit is as hot as a big river at its limit. Rivers and flood gauges both count — whichever is further up its scale wins the spot.

        The colour is the band it has crossed: yellow past its alert mark, orange past warning, red at danger. The same three marks the station's own meter draws.

        A flood gauge that has tripped goes straight to red — water standing over a spot known to flood is something observed, not forecast.

        Below the alert mark, nothing is drawn. A map that is warm everywhere says nothing.

        Rainfall has its own layer and scale; sirens and offline stations are not included.
